Amanita pantherina

Toxic woodland Amanita with striking white warts on a brown cap.

Morphological Snapshot

  • Cap: 5–12 cm, convex to flat, smooth brown surface adorned with white universal veil patches.
  • Gills: Free, white, and crowded.
  • Stipe: 6–12 cm, white with pendulous ring and bulbous base encircled by a prominent rim.

Habitat & Distribution

  • Region: Europe, Asia, and introduced populations in North America.
  • Associations: Forms ectomycorrhiza with conifers (spruce, fir, pine) and occasionally beech.
  • Fruiting Season: Summer through late autumn depending on rainfall.

Historical Context

Known to cause severe poisonings, Amanita pantherina has a cautionary reputation in European folklore. Modern field guides emphasize its distinction from edible Amanita species to prevent accidents.

Forager Notes

  • White gills and a ringed stalk should always trigger caution; dig around the base to observe volva features.
  • Amanita species accumulate toxins differently across regions; avoid experimentation.
  • Document coordinates and photograph specimens when reporting to poison control centers.
